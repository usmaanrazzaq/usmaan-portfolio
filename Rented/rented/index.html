Overview

What is Rented?

Peer-to-peer rentals for tools and everyday gear—without buying stuff you use once. I designed the mobile app end-to-end: onboarding, browse, rent, and list.

Problem

People buy tools and household items for one-off jobs, then store them—especially painful in small apartments. Store rental is heavy; classifieds are messy and low-trust. Rented aimed to make borrowing from neighbors feel as simple as shopping online.

My Role

Sole designer from scratch: onboarding, home/explore, product pages, and profiles. The hardest problem was browse and discovery that stayed legible across unrelated categories—from drills to tents.

Process

Research & Inspiration

I audited rental and marketplace apps (Hygglo, Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, OfferUp, Yoodlize) for onboarding, catalog structure, and first-time stranger trust. Most bury you in signup; few treat the same person as both renter and lister. Those gaps drove Rented's approach.

Information Architecture

Same user might rent Monday and list Tuesday. I mapped onboarding, rent, and list so the IA supported both without awkward mode switches. Onboarding stays light—email connect, no long forms—so download to browse stays under about a minute.

Exploration

In Figma I tried category grids, feeds, and a minimal home. With a thin catalog, minimal read as calm—not half-empty grids that erode trust. Profiles mixed marketplace utility (eBay, Grailed) with social familiarity (Instagram) for engineering's follow model: tabs for available, out on rent, and activity.

Prototyping & Testing

I shipped a Play 2 prototype runnable on real devices, with working fields so we could stress-test onboarding. Phone testing surfaced spacing and tap targets Figma didn't show; I iterated from what felt wrong in-hand.

Outcome & Reflection

Handoff: design system, annotated core flows, and an interactive prototype the team could run on their own phones.

We aligned on onboarding, profiles, and PDPs. The main ask was a simpler explore plus filter/sort for mixed inventory—which matched the tension I'd felt: multi-category browse is not a single-category shop.

Build paused when engineering reprioritized. The work still sharpened how I think about two-sided flows, heterogeneous catalogs, and small-team delivery.

If I had more time, I would:

  • Usability-test browse and onboarding with 5–8 target users pre-handoff.
  • Push explore further: filters, sorts, and tested layout variants.
  • Add trust signals—ratings, verification, transaction history—for stranger-to-stranger scale.
  • Bring the lister path up to the same polish as renting.
